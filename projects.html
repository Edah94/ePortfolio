DESCRIPTION

This short paper explores the potential of harnessing solar energy in urban communities within city of Salzburg. The study investigates the amount of generating electricity from incoming solar radiation using photovoltaic (PV) systems. The process involves in-depth exploration and utilization of the Area Solar Radiation (ASR) tool in ArcGIS to calculate solar radiation levels, which are then converted into electric power production data to assess the photovoltaic potential in these urban areas. The comprehensive investigation of the ASR method involved an extensive review of credible research papers and scholarly papers. This examination allowed me to gain a profound understanding of the method's qualities, including its complex calculations, data considerations, and the factors it includes or excludes, such as reflected radiation.

To estimate the PV potential, this geoprocessing involved steps like transferring the global radiation to the extent of building footprints, converting the Wh to more appropriate representation values, transforming solar radiation to electric power using a provided formula, and aggregating results to assess the total PV energy potential.

Throughout this course, in addition to this short paper, I have also grasped into creating structured research outline as part of the paper-writing process. Moreover, we enganged in the peer review process, providing valuable feedback on each other's research outlines. Finally, the course culminated in the creation of an elevator pitch presentation which conveyed the insights gained through GIS-based analysis.

Key areas: Solar radiation, photovoltaic potential, renewable energy, spatial analysis, GIS

The final assignment in Software Development aimed to create a Java-based solution for visualizing tweets in Google Earth. Additionally, the project's primary objectives were automation, modularity and user experience enhancement. It involved several key steps, including connecting to a Web Map Service (WMS), downloading a CSV file of tweets, converting tweets and WMS to KML format, dynamically creating color-coded polygons based on the time of tweet, and finally generating a Google Earth tour to guide users through the data.

To achieve modularity, the project was divided into multiple classes and methods, allowing for easy maintenance and debugging. User interaction was also a key focus, with prompts for directory selection, additional information viewing, and confirmation for launching the Google Earth.

DESCRIPTION

The final project in Spatial Databases course involved creating a SQL database to serve as the backend for a WebGIS application for a fictive festival. The objective was to organize festival content, including details about festival locations, their working hours, and the timing of stage events. The project was carried out using the tools like pgAdmin 4, QGIS, and MySQL Workbench.

Once the database structure was defined by designing a logical structure for the database, the implementation phase started by integrating geographic data into the database using QGIS. The data was connected to the database in Postgres management tool, establishing relationships between existing entities. Furtherly, SQL queries were performed to simulate a mobile application for festival visitors. Queries ranged from retrieving lists of festival locations with their details to determining which locations were currently open to querying whether a visitor was within the festival area and locating a nearest restroom.

The project not only equipped me with the skillsets to operate spatial database systems, use them for managing and analyzing spatial data, and understand the role of such systems in a GI context but also marked my first exposure to the SQL language, providing a valuable foundation for future data management and analysis endeavors.

Key areas: Database management, SQL queries, GIS
